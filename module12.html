Module 12: WLAN Concepts

Goal: Understand how wireless technology connects devices over radio frequencies.

12.1 Introduction to Wireless

Types of Wireless Networks

  • WPAN: Short range (Bluetooth, Zigbee).
  • WLAN: Medium range up to 300ft (Standard Wi-Fi, 802.11).
  • WMAN: City-wide area (WiMAX).
  • WWAN: Global or national (Cellular Broadband, Satellite).

Wi-Fi operates using radio frequencies in the 2.4 GHz and 5.0 GHz bands. It is standardized by the IEEE (which makes the rules) and the Wi-Fi Alliance (which ensures all devices work together).

12.2 WLAN Components

Access Points (APs)

Wireless Access Points provide wireless access to a wired network. There are two main categories:

  • Autonomous APs: Standalone routers (like the one in your home). You configure them manually, one by one.
  • Controller-based APs (LAPs): "Lightweight" APs. They require no initial configuration because they download all their settings from a central WLC (Wireless LAN Controller).

12.3 & 12.4 WLAN Operation & CAPWAP

Topology Modes & Access

Infrastructure Mode relies on an AP. One AP serving clients creates a BSS (Basic Service Set). Sticking multiple APs together via a wired connection creates an ESS (Extended Service Set).

Wireless is half-duplex (you can't send and receive at the same time). To avoid collisions, Wi-Fi uses CSMA/CA, meaning devices listen to the channel and wait for a clear opening before transmitting data.

How the WLC Controls APs (CAPWAP)

CAPWAP is the tunneling protocol the WLC uses to manage APs over IPv4 or IPv6. It uses DTLS encryption to secure the management traffic.

CAPWAP uses a Split MAC Architecture: The physical AP handles the fast radio jobs (like Beacons and probing), while the WLC handles the complex processing jobs (like Authentication and Roaming).

12.5 to 12.7 Wireless Security & Threats

Threats and Man-In-The-Middle

Hackers can use DoS attacks to jam frequencies, or plug in a Rogue AP. A common MITM attack is the Evil Twin Attack, where a hacker copies your network's SSID to steal traffic.

WLAN Security Methods

NEVER use WEP; it is broken. WPA2 using AES encryption is the standard today. WPA3 is the newest standard.

  • WPA2-Personal: Uses a Pre-Shared Key (PSK). Everyone uses the same Wi-Fi password.
  • WPA2-Enterprise: Uses an 802.1X/RADIUS Server. Every employee logs in with their own unique username and password.
